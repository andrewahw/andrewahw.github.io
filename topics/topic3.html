The Discrete Fourier Transform

Now we know more about Fourier Transforms, let's look at an actual example you can use.

The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) allows the transformation of an array of individual samples to another array of frequencies. To understand it, let's see the mathematical formulation of it:

Some groundwork

To fully understand this formulation, we first must understand complex numbers

Complex numbers can be imagined as a point on 2D space - with an "x" and "y" component. We express this in the form a + bi, where the "a" represents our x component, and the "b" our y component.

Another way of expressing complex numbers is by it's relation to the origin, 0. Generally we do this in the form a * e^bi, where "a" is the distance from the origin and "b" is the angle between the point and the origin in radians.
